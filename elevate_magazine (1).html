Contingent
The deal is moving forward provided specific conditions, like a clean inspection, are met.

The final milestone; the lender has approved all paperwork and you are ready to sign.

The seller will not make repairs, but you still maintain the right to inspect the property's condition.

Your last check of the home to ensure it is in the agreed-upon condition before funds transfer.

How to Choose
the Right Home

Start with what you can live with, not just what you like. The right home is one that still works for you years from now.

✦

It's easy to focus on finishes and upgrades. Countertops photograph beautifully. Staged lighting makes every room feel aspirational. But the right home is one that still works for you years from now — not just on day one.

1
Location always outlasts features
Paint, floors, and fixtures can change — but location cannot. Think about commute, daily routine, and how the area fits your lifestyle long-term. A beautiful kitchen in the wrong neighborhood is a compromise you'll feel every day.
2
Separate emotion from strategy
It's normal to love a home — but make sure the numbers, condition, and terms still make sense before deciding. Emotional decisions without strategic grounding cost buyers dearly in competitive markets.
3
Think resale, even if you're not planning to sell
Life changes. A strong future resale position gives you flexibility and protects your investment. The home you love today should be one others will love tomorrow.
4
Pay attention to flow, not just photos
Does the layout actually work for how you live? Online photos don't always reflect real-life movement through the home. Consider daily rhythms — morning routines, entertaining, quiet evenings.
5
Don't ignore the "feel" factor
Logic matters, but so does comfort. If a home checks the boxes but still doesn't feel right, that matters more than people admit. Trust your instincts alongside your analysis.

The right home isn't just found — it's recognized when strategy and lifestyle line up at the same time. That's where guidance makes the difference.

Finding a home is exciting, but the success is in managing everything that happens after the offer is accepted. That's where guidance matters most.

From accepted offer to closing day, here's exactly what happens next

Step 01
Offer Accepted
Your offer is accepted and the home moves from "available" to under contract. Earnest money is submitted and the process officially begins.
Step 02
Contract & Timeline Setup
All contract terms are finalized and key deadlines are set for inspections, appraisal, and closing.
Step 03
Home Inspection
A licensed inspector evaluates the property to identify any repairs or concerns before moving forward.
Step 04
Negotiations
Based on inspection results, repairs, credits, or adjustments may be requested and finalized between both parties.
Step 05
Appraisal
The lender confirms the home's value supports the purchase price.
Step 06
Loan Processing & Underwriting
Your lender reviews all financial documents and finalizes loan approval.
Step 07
Final Walkthrough
A last review of the home to ensure everything is in agreed condition before closing.
08

Closing Day — Documents are signed, funds are transferred, and ownership officially changes hands.

The Most Searched Questions

Top 3 Questions
Buyers Ask Online

✦
Question 01
How much house can I afford?
It's not just about the price — it's about your monthly payment. Most buyers qualify based on income, debts, and interest rates. A quick pre-approval will show you a comfortable price range so you can shop with confidence, not guesswork.
Question 02
What credit score do I need?
You might be closer than you think. Many loan programs allow scores as low as the mid-500s to low-600s, depending on the loan type. Even if you're not quite ready, a lender can guide you on simple steps to improve quickly.
Question 03
Is now a good time to buy?
The right time depends on you, not the headlines. If you're financially ready, today's market can offer opportunities like less competition or negotiating power. Plus, you can always refinance later if rates improve.
Mistakes Buyers Make
  • Starting the search before getting pre-approved
  • Focusing only on the listing price
  • Waiting too long to make an offer
  • Skipping strategy in competitive situations
  • Overlooking inspection results emotionally

Understanding Your Investment

Transaction
Cost

Understanding beyond the down payment.

Up front and
out of pocket

These are paid during the under-contract period and are separate from your final closing funds.

Earnest Money Deposit (EMD)
A good-faith deposit credited toward your total down payment at settlement.
Inspections
The home inspection and any necessary pest, mold or other inspections are paid directly to the inspector to uncover potential issues and provide negotiation leverage.
Appraisal Fee
Required by lenders to confirm the property value; typically paid up-front at the lender's request.
Closing cost
break down

In an attorney state like South Carolina, your final settlement includes:

Lender Fees
Costs to process and underwrite your loan.
Title Services
Required Lender's policy and recommended Owner's Title Insurance for your protection.
Closing Attorney Fee
South Carolina law requires an attorney to manage the process and settlement meeting.
Recording Fees & Taxes
Fees paid to the county to record the deed and state transfer taxes (Doc Stamps).
Escrow Pre-paids
A reserve for property taxes and your first year of homeowner's insurance.

Funds required for closing

These remaining funds are due at the closing table, as detailed on your final Closing Disclosure (CD).

  • Down Payment: Your total purchase price minus the base loan amount (your initial EMD is deducted from this total).
  • Closing Costs: Generally ranging from 2% to 5% of the purchase price.

Pre-Qualification:
The Starting Line

Your search for the discerning starts with understanding your purchasing power. Consider pre-qualification your baseline — a preliminary assessment of what you might be able to afford, giving you a clearer picture of the path forward in a competitive landscape like South Carolina.

  • Basis Self-reported income and debt.
  • Speed Instant, informal estimate.
  • Purpose Establishes a rough baseline budget.
  • Market Weight Low; intended for initial planning.
The Gold Standard
Pre-Approval:
The Gold Standard

While pre-qualification is an estimate, pre-approval is the verified assurance sellers look for. In South Carolina's competitive market, arriving pre-approved signals you are a serious, "Buyer Ready" offer — and that distinction can make all the difference.

  • Basis Verified tax returns, pay stubs, and credit.
  • Speed Comprehensive underwriting review.
  • Purpose Secures a formal loan commitment letter.
  • Market Weight High; signals a serious, "Buyer Ready" offer.

Winning
the Right
Home

Finding the right home is only the beginning. Securing it on the right terms, while protecting your long-term investment, is where strategy becomes essential. In today's market, success is rarely determined by price alone — it is determined by structure, timing, and the ability to evaluate opportunity with clarity rather than emotion.

The strongest offers are not always the highest — they are the most intentional.

Value over emotion

Decisions should be grounded in market reality, not competition or urgency.

Strength over speed

Clean financing, strong terms, and reduced risk often carry more weight than price alone.

Leverage in structure

Flexibility with closing timelines, contingencies, and seller priorities can position an offer more competitively without increasing cost.

Disciplined decision-making

Not every home should be pursued. The right move includes knowing when to proceed — and when to step back.

Understanding Potential Risks

Every property presents opportunity, but also requires careful evaluation. The goal is not avoidance — it is awareness.


Key areas that require attention include structural and long-term considerations such as foundation integrity, roof condition, flood exposure, and any unpermitted improvements.


Each of these factors can influence not only cost, but also insurability, resale value, and future flexibility.

What Can Impact Your Closing Timeline

Even well-structured transactions can experience delays once under contract. Understanding this upfront helps prevent unnecessary stress or rushed decisions.


Common factors include appraisal variances, additional lender documentation, title findings, or inspection-related negotiations. During this phase, financial stability is essential — significant purchases, new debt, or employment changes can directly affect loan approval.

A Realistic Timeline

Most transactions move from contract to closing in approximately 30 to 45 days, depending on financing and property-specific factors.


A measured approach supports a smoother experience: avoid committing to move-out dates too early, delay non-essential scheduling until final approval, and maintain flexibility through closing.
